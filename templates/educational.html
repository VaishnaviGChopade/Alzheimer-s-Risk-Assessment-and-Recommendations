Alzheimer's is a progressive brain disorder that slowly destroys memory, thinking skills, and eventually the ability to carry out simple tasks.

Common early signs include memory loss, confusion with time or place, difficulty completing familiar tasks, and trouble finding the right words.

No. Dementia is an umbrella term for memory loss and cognitive decline; Alzheimer’s is the most common type of dementia.

While rare, early-onset Alzheimer’s can affect people under 65, sometimes as early as their 30s or 40s.

Currently, there is no cure. However, treatments and lifestyle changes can slow progression and improve quality of life.

The exact cause is unknown, but it's linked to a combination of genetics, lifestyle, and environmental factors that affect the brain over time.

Genetics can play a role, especially in early-onset cases. However, lifestyle and environment are also key factors in most cases.

Doctors diagnose Alzheimer’s through cognitive tests, medical history, brain imaging (like MRI/CT), and sometimes lab tests.

Alzheimer’s typically progresses through early, middle, and late stages, with increasing levels of memory loss and dependence.

Normal aging may involve some forgetfulness, but Alzheimer’s causes consistent memory decline and difficulty performing daily tasks.
